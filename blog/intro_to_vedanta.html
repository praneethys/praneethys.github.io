The Six Schools of Vedanta

Vedanta, meaning "the end of the Vedas i.e., complete knowledge of the Veda," represents the philosophical conclusions drawn from the Upanishads. Over centuries, different teachers interpreted these ancient texts in their own ways, leading to the formation of distinct schools of thought. While they all draw from the same source texts primarily the Upanishads, Brahma Sutras, and Bhagavad Gita their conclusions about the nature of reality, the self, and liberation (moksha) vary quite dramatically.

What I find fascinating is how each school emerged as a response to the interpretations that came before it. They weren't created in isolation but through vigorous debate and philosophical inquiry. Here's my understanding of the six major schools:

The Major Schools

Vedanta Philosophy
Non-Dualistic Schools

Schools that emphasize the oneness or fundamental non-difference between the individual soul and ultimate reality.

Advaita Vedanta (अद्वैत वेदान्त)
Founder: Adi Shankaracharya (8th century CE)
Radical non-dualism: only Brahman exists, everything else is maya (illusion). The individual soul (atman) and universal soul (Brahman) are identical. Liberation comes through knowledge (jnana) alone.
Shuddhadvaita (Pure Non-Dualism)
Founder: Vallabhacharya (15th-16th century CE)
Accepts non-dualism but rejects maya as illusion. Everything is real. Brahman (Krishna) manifests as the universe through divine will. The world is Krishna's lila (divine play). Liberation through pushti-bhakti (grace-nourished devotion).
Qualified Non-Dualistic Schools

Schools that propose both unity and distinction between the soul and Godparadoxical yet simultaneous.

Vishishtadvaita (Qualified Non-Dualism)
Founder: Ramanuja (11th-12th century CE)
Brahman, world, and souls are all real but not independent. The world and souls form the body of Brahman, inseparable yet distinct. Liberation through bhakti combined with knowledge. Souls retain individuality even in moksha.
Dvaitadvaita (Dualistic Non-Dualism)
Founder: Nimbarka (13th century CE)
Bhedabheda philosophy: the soul is both different from and not different from Brahman simultaneously. Krishna is the supreme form of Brahman. Natural, inseparable relationship between God and souls. Radha-Krishna devotion as the path.
Achintya Bhedabheda
Founder: Chaitanya Mahaprabhu (16th century CE)
The relationship between soul and God is achintya (inconceivable) to the human mind. We're both one with and different from Krishna, beyond logic. Emphasis on nama-sankirtana (chanting). Bhakti-rasa (devotional mellows) as highest spiritual experience.
Dualistic School

School that emphasizes fundamental, eternal differences between God, souls, and matter.

Dvaita (Dualism)
Founder: Madhvacharya (13th century CE)
Fundamental, eternal differences between God and souls, soul and matter, different souls, and different material objects. God (Vishnu) is utterly transcendent and supreme. Five fundamental differences (pancha-bheda). Liberation is experiencing God's grace, not merging.

Key Texts Common to All Schools

While each school has its own commentaries and texts, they all reference three foundational texts known as the Prasthanatrayi (Triple Canon):

Upanishads

The philosophical portions of the Vedas, containing direct teachings about the nature of reality, self, and Brahman.

Brahma Sutras

Also called Vedanta Sutras, systematically organizes the teachings of the Upanishads. Each school has its own commentary (bhashya).

Bhagavad Gita

Krishna's teachings to Arjuna, synthesizing various paths to liberation including karma, jnana, and bhakti yoga.

Final Thoughts

What strikes me about these schools is that they're all trying to answer the same fundamental questions: Who am I? What is reality? How do I find liberation? But the diversity of their answers shows the richness of Vedantic inquiry. Shankara emphasizes pure knowledge, Ramanuja balances devotion and knowledge, Madhva centers on complete surrender to a transcendent God, and the later teachers explore various nuances of relationship with the divine.

Each school has produced saints, scholars, and practitioners who found meaning and liberation through their respective paths. There's no universally accepted "correct" interpretation which, honestly, might be the beauty of it all.

I'm still learning and my understanding keeps evolving. If you're interested in diving deeper, I'd recommend reading the original commentaries of these teachers rather than relying solely on secondary sources. The depth and subtlety of their arguments is something you only appreciate when you engage directly with their texts.
