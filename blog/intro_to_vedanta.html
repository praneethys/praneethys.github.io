The Six Schools of Vedanta

Vedanta, meaning "the end of the Vedas i.e., complete knowledge of the Veda," represents the philosophical conclusions drawn from the Upanishads. Over centuries, different teachers interpreted these ancient texts in their own ways, leading to the formation of distinct schools of thought. While they all draw from the same source textsprimarily the Upanishads, Brahma Sutras, and Bhagavad Gitatheir conclusions about the nature of reality, the self, and liberation (moksha) vary quite dramatically.

What I find fascinating is how each school emerged as a response to the interpretations that came before it. They weren't created in isolation but through vigorous debate and philosophical inquiry. Here's my understanding of the six major schools:

The Major Schools

Vedanta Philosophy
Non-Dualistic Schools

Schools that emphasize the oneness or fundamental non-difference between the individual soul and ultimate reality.

Advaita Vedanta (अद्वैत वेदान्त)
Founder: Adi Shankaracharya (8th century CE)
Radical non-dualism: only Brahman exists, everything else is maya (illusion). The individual soul (atman) and universal soul (Brahman) are identical. Liberation comes through knowledge (jnana) alone.
Shuddhadvaita (Pure Non-Dualism)
Founder: Vallabhacharya (15th-16th century CE)
Accepts non-dualism but rejects maya as illusion. Everything is realBrahman (Krishna) manifests as the universe through divine will. The world is Krishna's lila (divine play). Liberation through pushti-bhakti (grace-nourished devotion).
Qualified Non-Dualistic Schools

Schools that propose both unity and distinction between the soul and Godparadoxical yet simultaneous.

Vishishtadvaita (Qualified Non-Dualism)
Founder: Ramanuja (11th-12th century CE)
Brahman, world, and souls are all real but not independent. The world and souls form the body of Brahman, inseparable yet distinct. Liberation through bhakti combined with knowledge. Souls retain individuality even in moksha.
Dvaitadvaita (Dualistic Non-Dualism)
Founder: Nimbarka (13th century CE)
Bhedabheda philosophy: the soul is both different from and not different from Brahman simultaneously. Krishna is the supreme form of Brahman. Natural, inseparable relationship between God and souls. Radha-Krishna devotion as the path.
Achintya Bhedabheda
Founder: Chaitanya Mahaprabhu (16th century CE)
The relationship between soul and God is achintyainconceivable to the human mind. We're both one with and different from Krishna, beyond logic. Emphasis on nama-sankirtana (chanting). Bhakti-rasa (devotional mellows) as highest spiritual experience.
Dualistic School

School that emphasizes fundamental, eternal differences between God, souls, and matter.

Dvaita (Dualism)
Founder: Madhvacharya (13th century CE)
Fundamental, eternal differences between God and souls, soul and matter, different souls, and different material objects. God (Vishnu) is utterly transcendent and supreme. Five fundamental differences (pancha-bheda). Liberation is experiencing God's grace, not merging.

Key Texts Common to All Schools

While each school has its own commentaries and texts, they all reference three foundational texts known as the Prasthanatrayi (Triple Canon):

Upanishads

The philosophical portions of the Vedas, containing direct teachings about the nature of reality, self, and Brahman.

Brahma Sutras

Also called Vedanta Sutras, systematically organizes the teachings of the Upanishads. Each school has its own commentary (bhashya).

Bhagavad Gita

Krishna's teachings to Arjuna, synthesizing various paths to liberation including karma, jnana, and bhakti yoga.

Detailed School Overview

Advaita Vedanta (अद्वैत वेदान्त) - Non-Dualism

Founded by: Adi Shankaracharya (8th century CE)

Probably the most well-known school in the West, Advaita takes a radical stance: there is only one reality, Brahman, and everything else is maya (illusion). The individual soul (atman) and the universal soul (Brahman) are not two different thingsthey're identical. Our perception of separateness is just ignorance.

Key Texts:

  • Brahma Sutra Bhashya (Shankara's commentary on Brahma Sutras)
  • Vivekachudamani (Crest-Jewel of Discrimination)
  • Upadesha Sahasri (A Thousand Teachings)
  • Various Upanishad commentaries

Core Concepts:

  • Brahman is the only reality; the world is mithya (neither real nor unreal)
  • Jiva (individual soul) and Brahman are one and the same
  • Liberation comes through knowledge (jnana) alone
  • The concept of adhyasa (superimposition) we mistakenly project qualities onto Brahman

Vishishtadvaita - Qualified Non-Dualism

Founded by: Ramanuja (11th-12th century CE)

Ramanuja couldn't accept Shankara's idea that the world is just an illusion. He argued for a more inclusive view: Brahman is real, the world is real, and individual souls are real. Butand this is the keythey're not independent. The world and souls form the body of Brahman, inseparable yet distinct. Think of it like how your body and soul are different but not separate.

Key Texts:

  • Sri Bhashya (commentary on Brahma Sutras)
  • Vedartha Sangraha
  • Gita Bhashya (commentary on Bhagavad Gita)

Core Concepts:

  • Brahman is qualified by the world and souls (hence "qualified" non-dualism)
  • Three realities: Ishwara (God), jiva (souls), and jagat (world)
  • Liberation through bhakti (devotion) combined with knowledge
  • Souls retain their individuality even in moksha

Dvaita - Dualism

Founded by: Madhvacharya (13th century CE)

Madhva went even further than Ramanuja in emphasizing distinctness. For him, there are fundamental, eternal differencesbetween God and souls, between soul and matter, between different souls, and even between different material objects. God (Vishnu) is utterly transcendent and supreme. We are eternally separate from and dependent on Him.

Key Texts:

  • Brahma Sutra Bhashya
  • Anuvyakhyana (a commentary on his own Brahma Sutra Bhashya)
  • Mahabharata Tatparya Nirnaya
  • Bhagavata Tatparya Nirnaya

Core Concepts:

  • Five fundamental differences (pancha-bheda)
  • God, souls, and matter are eternally distinct
  • Souls are inherently graded in quality (taratamya)
  • Liberation is experiencing God's grace, not merging with God

Dvaitadvaita - Dualistic Non-Dualism

Founded by: Nimbarka (13th century CE, though dates are debated)

Nimbarka tried to reconcile the dualist and non-dualist viewpoints. His philosophy says that the soul is both different from and not different from Brahmansimultaneously. It's paradoxical, sure, but he argued that's just the nature of reality. God, souls, and matter are distinct, yet there's an inseparable relationship between them.

Key Texts:

  • Vedanta Parijata Saurabha (commentary on Brahma Sutras)
  • Madhva-Mukha-Mardana
  • Commentaries by his disciple Shrinivasa

Core Concepts:

  • Bhedabheda (difference and non-difference) simultaneously
  • Krishna is the supreme form of Brahman
  • Radha-Krishna devotion as the path to liberation
  • Natural, inseparable relationship between God and souls

Shuddhadvaita - Pure Non-Dualism

Founded by: Vallabhacharya (15th-16th century CE)

Vallabha's philosophy is interesting because he accepts non-dualism but rejects the idea of maya as illusion. For him, everything is realBrahman, in the form of Krishna, manifests as the universe through his own will. The world isn't an illusion or a mistake; it's Krishna's lila (divine play). Souls are parts of Brahman that have become covered by ignorance, but they're never fundamentally separate.

Key Texts:

  • Anubhashya (commentary on Brahma Sutras)
  • Subodhini (commentary on Bhagavata Purana)
  • Shodasha Grantha (sixteen philosophical treatises)

Core Concepts:

  • Brahman is the only realityno maya doctrine
  • Krishna is the highest form of Brahman
  • Pushti (divine grace) is essential for liberation
  • Path of pushti-bhakti (devotion nourished by grace)

Achintya Bhedabheda - Inconceivable Difference and Non-Difference

Founded by: Chaitanya Mahaprabhu (16th century CE), systematized by his followers

Chaitanya Mahaprabhu didn't write philosophical texts himselfhe was more focused on devotional practice through kirtan and chanting. But his followers, particularly Jiva Goswami and the Six Goswamis of Vrindavan, developed his teachings into a sophisticated philosophy. The core idea is that the relationship between the soul and God is achintyainconceivable to the human mind. We can't fully grasp how we're both one with and different from Krishna.

Key Texts:

  • Sat Sandarbhas (Six Sandarbhas) by Jiva Goswami
  • Chaitanya Charitamrita by Krishnadasa Kaviraja
  • Writings of the Six Goswamis

Core Concepts:

  • Relationship with God is simultaneously one and different, beyond logic
  • Krishna is the supreme personality of Godhead
  • Emphasis on nama-sankirtana (chanting the holy names)
  • Bhakti-rasa (devotional mellows) as the highest spiritual experience

Final Thoughts

What strikes me about these schools is that they're all trying to answer the same fundamental questions: Who am I? What is reality? How do I find liberation? But the diversity of their answers shows the richness of Vedantic inquiry. Shankara emphasizes pure knowledge, Ramanuja balances devotion and knowledge, Madhva centers on complete surrender to a transcendent God, and the later teachers explore various nuances of relationship with the divine.

Each school has produced saints, scholars, and practitioners who found meaning and liberation through their respective paths. There's no universally accepted "correct" interpretationwhich, honestly, might be the beauty of it all.

I'm still learning and my understanding keeps evolving. If you're interested in diving deeper, I'd recommend reading the original commentaries of these teachers rather than relying solely on secondary sources. The depth and subtlety of their arguments is something you only appreciate when you engage directly with their texts.
