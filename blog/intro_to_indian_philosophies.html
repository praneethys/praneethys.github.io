Introduction to Indian Philosophies

Indian philosophy has a rich and diverse tradition spanning thousands of years. The philosophical systems developed in ancient India can be broadly classified into two major categories based on their acceptance or rejection of the Vedas as a source of knowledge.

Before diving into these schools, it's essential to understand the foundational concept that underlies much of Indian philosophical thought: the Purusharthas, or the goals of human life.

Purushartha: The Four Goals of Life

A key concept in understanding the structure of Indian philosophies is that of purushartha - the goals of mankind. These four objectives provide the framework for understanding what Indian philosophy considers the complete human experience:

1. Dharma

To lead an ethical and righteous life. Dharma encompasses duty, moral law, and the principle that sustains the cosmic and social order.

2. Artha

To acquire wealth, position, and social or political status. This represents the material and worldly pursuits necessary for a meaningful life.

3. Kama

To fulfill all other desires, including sensual and aesthetic pleasures. This acknowledges the importance of desire and enjoyment in human life.

4. Moksha

Salvation or liberation from the cycle of birth and death. This deals not with life in this world, but with the eternal destiny of the individual.

The Two Major Branches

Indian Philosophies
Astika (Orthodox)

Accept the Vedas as a valid source of knowledge about dharma and moksha. Traditionally counted as six schools (Shad Darshana), usually grouped in pairs.

Nyaya & Vaisheshika
Nyaya focuses on logic, epistemology, and valid reasoning. Vaisheshika presents an atomic theory, analyzing reality into categories (padarthas). Together they provide the logical tools and metaphysical framework for understanding reality.
Yoga & Samkhya
Samkhya is one of the oldest systems, presenting dualistic metaphysics of purusha (consciousness) and prakriti (matter). Yoga, systematized by Patanjali, provides the practical methodology for achieving liberation. Theory meets practice.
Purva Mimamsa & Uttara Mimamsa (Vedanta)
Purva Mimamsa focuses on Vedic rituals and dharma, emphasizing proper conduct. Uttara Mimamsa (Vedanta) focuses on the Upanishads, dealing primarily with moksha and the nature of ultimate reality. → Learn more about Vedanta schools
Nastika (Heterodox)

Explicitly reject the Vedas as an independently valid source of knowledge about dharma and moksha. Developed their own epistemologies and paths to liberation.

Lokayata or Charvaka
The materialist school that accepts only perception as valid knowledge. Denies the afterlife, karma, and moksha, advocating pursuit of pleasure in this life. Most original texts lost; we know them primarily through critiques by other schools.
Bauddha (Buddhism)
Founded by Gautama Buddha. Core concepts include the Four Noble Truths, Eightfold Path, dependent origination (pratityasamutpada), and no-self (anatman). Major schools: Theravada, Mahayana, and Vajrayana, each with their own sub-schools and interpretations.
Jaina or Syadvada
Emphasizes non-violence (ahimsa), many-sidedness of truth (anekantavada), and "maybe" doctrine (syadvada). Liberation comes through right faith, knowledge, and conduct. Split into Digambara (sky-clad) and Svetambara (white-clad) groups.

Understanding the Classification

The distinction between orthodox and heterodox isn't about being "right" or "wrong"—it's simply a way of categorizing schools based on their relationship to Vedic authority. Many heterodox schools like Buddhism and Jainism share significant philosophical ground with orthodox schools, and there was extensive debate and mutual influence among all these traditions.

What makes Indian philosophy fascinating is this diversity of viewpoints, all rigorously argued and systematically developed. Each school offers a unique perspective on fundamental questions about reality, knowledge, ethics, and liberation. Understanding this landscape gives you a foundation for exploring any particular school in depth.
